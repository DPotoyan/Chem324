Classical Mechanics: From Newton to Hamilton

What you will learn

  • Newton’s mechanics describes motion with forces. F=maF = maF=ma predicts trajectories once you know the forces and the starting conditions.

  • The Hamiltonian reformulation describes motion with energy. The same physics is repackaged around the total energy H=T+VH = T + VH=T+V and the pair of variables position qqq and momentum ppp.

  • Phase space is the natural arena. A classical state is a single point (q,p)(q, p)(q,p), and its motion traces a curve of constant energy.

  • The Hamiltonian is the doorway to quantum mechanics. Replacing position and momentum by operators turns HHH into the quantum Hamiltonian H^\hat HH^ and Hamilton’s equations into the Schrodinger equation.

Before quantum mechanics we need one idea from classical physics done properly: the Hamiltonian. Newton’s laws are what you learned first, but quantum mechanics is not built on forces. It is built on energy, on the variables position and momentum, and on a bracket that becomes the commutator. This page explains why we reformulate classical mechanics and how that reformulation hands us the Schrodinger equation.

Newton’s mechanics¶

Newton’s second law says a force changes a particle’s momentum:

Newton’s second law
F=ma=md2xdt2F = ma = m\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2}F=ma=mdt2d2x​
(1)

Give the force and the initial position and velocity, and the trajectory x(t)x(t)x(t) is fixed for all time. For a mass on a spring the force is F=−kxF = -kxF=−kx, giving oscillation x(t)=Acos⁡(ωt)x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)x(t)=Acos(ωt) with ω=k/m\omega = \sqrt{k/m}ω=k/m​.

Two quantities organize the motion. The kinetic energy T=12mv2T = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2T=21​mv2 and the potential energy V(x)V(x)V(x), whose negative slope is the force, F=−dV/dxF = -dV/dxF=−dV/dx. Their sum, the total energy, stays constant as the particle moves:

E=T+V=12mv2+V(x)=constant.E = T + V = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + V(x) = \text{constant}.E=T+V=21​mv2+V(x)=constant.
(2)

Newton’s picture is intuitive, but it is awkward for quantum mechanics: it centers on force, a vector that is hard to generalize to abstract coordinates, and it hides the energy, which is the quantity quantum mechanics actually quantizes. We want a formulation built directly on energy.

The Hamiltonian reformulation¶

The trick is to stop using position and velocity, and instead use position qqq and momentum p=mvp = mvp=mv as the two independent variables. The Hamiltonian is the total energy written in terms of these:

The Hamiltonian
H(q,p)=T+V=p22m+V(q)H(q, p) = T + V = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(q)H(q,p)=T+V=2mp2​+V(q)
(3)

All of the dynamics now follows from a single, strikingly symmetric pair of first-order equations:

Hamilton’s equations of motion
dqdt=∂H∂p,dpdt=−∂H∂q\frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \frac{dp}{dt} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}dtdq​=∂p∂H​,dtdp​=−∂q∂H​
(4)

The first equation just restates p=mq˙p = m\dot qp=mq˙​; the second reproduces Newton’s law, since −∂H/∂q=−dV/dq=F-\partial H/\partial q = -dV/dq = F−∂H/∂q=−dV/dq=F. So Hamilton’s equations contain exactly the same physics as F=maF = maF=ma, only now written symmetrically in qqq and ppp and driven entirely by the energy function HHH.

Where the Lagrangian fits

Between Newton and Hamilton sits the Lagrangian L=T−VL = T - VL=T−V, whose Euler-Lagrange equation ddt∂L∂q˙=∂L∂q\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q}dtd​∂q˙​∂L​=∂q∂L​ follows from the principle of least action: the actual path makes the action S=∫L dtS = \int L\,dtS=∫Ldt stationary. The Hamiltonian is obtained from the Lagrangian by switching from velocity q˙\dot qq˙​ to momentum p=∂L/∂q˙p = \partial L/\partial \dot qp=∂L/∂q˙​. We go straight to the Hamiltonian because it, not the Lagrangian, is what quantum mechanics quantizes. The least-action idea does return in Feynman’s path-integral formulation.

Phase space¶

In the Hamiltonian picture a complete classical state is a single point (q,p)(q, p)(q,p) in phase space, the plane of position versus momentum. As time runs, the point moves along a curve, and because energy is conserved that curve is a contour of constant HHH.

For the harmonic oscillator H=p22m+12mω2q2H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2H=2mp2​+21​mω2q2, the constant-energy contours are ellipses. A low-energy state traces a small ellipse near the origin; higher energy means a bigger ellipse.

Source
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

m, omega = 1.0, 1.0
q = np.linspace(-3, 3, 400)
p = np.linspace(-3, 3, 400)
Q, P = np.meshgrid(q, p)
H = P**2 / (2 * m) + 0.5 * m * omega**2 * Q**2

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(11, 4.2))

# Phase-space energy contours (ellipses)
levels = [0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 1.8, 2.8]
cs = axes[0].contour(Q, P, H, levels=levels, cmap='viridis')
axes[0].clabel(cs, fmt='E=%.1f', fontsize=8)
# One trajectory, arrow shows clockwise flow
E = 1.0
theta = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 200)
axes[0].plot(np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.cos(theta), np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.sin(theta), 'k', lw=1)
axes[0].annotate('', xy=(np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.cos(0.3), np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.sin(0.3)),
                 xytext=(np.sqrt(2 * E), 0.0),
                 arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', color='k'))
axes[0].set_xlabel('position q')
axes[0].set_ylabel('momentum p')
axes[0].set_aspect('equal')
axes[0].set_title('Fig.1a Phase space: energy contours are ellipses')

# Time view: position and momentum oscillate, 90 deg out of phase
t = np.linspace(0, 4 * np.pi, 400)
axes[1].plot(t, np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.cos(t), color='#2e4057', lw=2, label='q(t)')
axes[1].plot(t, -np.sqrt(2 * E) * np.sin(t), color='#d1495b', lw=2, label='p(t)')
axes[1].axhline(0, color='gray', lw=0.6)
axes[1].set_xlabel('time t')
axes[1].legend(fontsize=9)
axes[1].set_title('Fig.1b Newton view: q and p oscillate in time')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The two panels are the same motion in two languages: Hamilton’s phase-space ellipse (left) and Newton’s time trajectories (right). Quantum mechanics will replace the sharp phase-space point with a spread-out probability cloud, because position and momentum can no longer be pinned down at once.

Why the Hamiltonian is the bridge to quantum mechanics¶

Three features of the Hamiltonian formulation carry over almost verbatim into quantum mechanics.

Energy is the central object. Quantum mechanics quantizes energy, and HHH is the energy. The quantum Hamiltonian H^\hat HH^ is what appears in the Schrodinger equation, and its eigenvalues are the allowed energy levels.

States and observables. A classical observable is any function of (q,p)(q, p)(q,p): position is qqq, momentum is ppp, energy is H(q,p)H(q,p)H(q,p). In quantum mechanics each becomes an operator, and the recipe (called canonical quantization) is direct:

Canonical quantization
q  →  x^=x,p  →  p^=−iℏ∂∂x,q \;\to\; \hat x = x, \qquad p \;\to\; \hat p = -i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial x},q→x^=x,p→p^​=−iℏ∂x∂​,
(5)
H=p22m+V(q)  →  H^=−ℏ22m∂2∂x2+V(x).H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(q) \;\to\; \hat H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x).H=2mp2​+V(q)→H^=−2mℏ2​∂x2∂2​+V(x).
(6)

Feeding this H^\hat HH^ into iℏ ∂tΨ=H^Ψi\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi = \hat H\Psiiℏ∂t​Ψ=H^Ψ gives the Schrodinger equation. The classical Hamiltonian literally becomes the quantum Hamiltonian by substituting operators for qqq and ppp.

The Poisson bracket becomes the commutator. Classical mechanics has a bracket that measures how two observables co-vary,

{A,B}=∂A∂q∂B∂p−∂A∂p∂B∂q,so{q,p}=1.\{A, B\} = \frac{\partial A}{\partial q}\frac{\partial B}{\partial p} - \frac{\partial A}{\partial p}\frac{\partial B}{\partial q}, \qquad \text{so}\quad \{q, p\} = 1.{A,B}=∂q∂A​∂p∂B​−∂p∂A​∂q∂B​,so{q,p}=1.
(7)

Quantization promotes this bracket to the commutator through the rule {A,B}→1iℏ[A^,B^]\{A,B\} \to \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat A, \hat B]{A,B}→iℏ1​[A^,B^], which turns {q,p}=1\{q,p\}=1{q,p}=1 into

The canonical commutation relation
[x^,p^]=x^p^−p^x^=iℏ[\hat x, \hat p] = \hat x\hat p - \hat p\hat x = i\hbar[x^,p^​]=x^p^​−p^​x^=iℏ
(8)

This single non-zero commutator is the seed of the uncertainty principle: because x^\hat xx^ and p^\hat pp^​ do not commute, no state can have a definite position and a definite momentum at the same time. The operators as matrices page showed why non-commuting operators behave this way; here we see where that particular commutator comes from.

Example: the harmonic oscillator, classical to quantum

Classically H=p22m+12mω2x2H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2H=2mp2​+21​mω2x2, with any energy E≥0E \geq 0E≥0 allowed and phase-space ellipses of any size. Quantizing with p→−iℏ ∂xp \to -i\hbar\,\partial_xp→−iℏ∂x​ gives

H^=−ℏ22md2dx2+12mω2x2,\hat H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2,H^=−2mℏ2​dx2d2​+21​mω2x2,
(9)

whose eigenvalues are the discrete ladder En=ℏω(n+12)E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)En​=ℏω(n+21​). Same Hamiltonian, but the commutator [x^,p^]=iℏ[\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar[x^,p^​]=iℏ forces the energy to come in quantized steps with a nonzero ground state. This is the model system you meet in Chapter 4.
