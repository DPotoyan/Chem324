The need for quantization

What you will learn.

  • The energies of atoms, molecules, and light are quantized. This means that energy can only assume specific, discrete values rather than a continuous range.

  • Energy quantization is a fundamental principle of our physical reality, consistently observed in quantum experiments. Quantum mechanics provides a comprehensive explanation and accurate predictions of this phenomenon.

  • Key historical developments leading to the emergence of quantum mechanics include black body radiation, the double slit experiment, and the photoelectric effect.

  • Several macroscopic phenomena, such as the red glow of hot metals, the heat capacity of solids at low temperatures, and the colors of materials, are all manifestations of quantum effects.

What is the nature of light?¶

  • According to classical wave theory, light is seen as a traveling wave consisting of electric and magnetic components.

  • We will soon see that this picture of light as an electromagnetic wave is not the whole story, and radically new ideas are needed to understand a wide variety of phenomena involving the interaction of light with atoms and molecules.

Spectrum of electromagnetic waves¶

  • Visible light occupies a narrow frequency region in between.

  • High-frequency waves carry much higher energy. This means X-rays or gamma rays can only be generated by heating “stuff” up to very high temperatures. This happens naturally at the core of the Sun!

  • Low-frequency waves carry less energy. They can be generated in a “microwave” or by broadcasting antennas.

  • So what is the relationship between the frequency of radiation ν\nuν and its energy EEE? This is not such a trivial question. In fact, this very question arose in connection with black body radiation, an experiment that forever changed the course of history by giving birth to quantum mechanics!

Black body as an idealized model¶

Definition of black body

A black body is an idealized model, just like the ideal gas model in thermodynamics. It is assumed to be in thermodynamic equilibrium, maintained at some constant temperature TTT, and it both absorbs and emits every wavelength of electromagnetic radiation.

  • This model is called a black body because it absorbs every wavelength that hits its surface, therefore appearing as a perfectly black object.

  • If an object has a color, it is because it reflects certain wavelengths of light, which are then detected by the retina of our eye. The distribution of wavelengths emitted by a black body is determined only by its temperature!

Ultraviolet catastrophe of classical mechanics¶

  • What is radiation in classical mechanics? Radiation is considered a wave with frequency ν\nuν. In a heated body, naturally vibrating springs (which represent atoms or molecules) generate waves with the same frequency.

  • Packing wave modes in a box. One can fit more high-frequency (short-wavelength) waves in the box than low-frequency ones. The number of waves we can fit in a cubic box in the frequency region [ν,ν+dν][\nu, \nu+d\nu][ν,ν+dν] can be estimated to be ∼dν3∼ν2dν\sim d \nu^3 \sim \nu^2 d\nu∼dν3∼ν2dν. The constant of proportionality requires a few more steps to derive, which we skip, writing only the final result:

dNν=8πc3⋅ν2dνdN_{\nu} = \frac{8\pi}{c^3} \cdot \nu^2 d\nudNν​=c38π​⋅ν2dν
(2)

  • Equipartition of energy. From thermodynamics we know that in equilibrium each degree of freedom, or each oscillator, gets the same energy kBTk_BTkB​T, where kBk_BkB​ is the Boltzmann constant.

⟨E⟩=kBT\langle E\rangle = k_BT⟨E⟩=kB​T
(3)

  • Every vibrating spring in a heated body thus has the same energy regardless of frequency. Think about this assumption for a second!

  • Radiation energy distribution. The distribution of radiated frequencies is then the product of the average thermal energy and the number of springs in a frequency interval:

ρ(ν)=kBT⋅8πc3ν2\rho({\nu}) = k_B T \cdot \frac{8\pi}{c^3}\nu^2ρ(ν)=kB​T⋅c38π​ν2
(4)

  • Ultraviolet catastrophe. The energy distribution shoots to infinity at high ν\nuν (or low λ\lambdaλ). This is known as the ultraviolet catastrophe! Integrating ρ\rhoρ over all frequencies gives the total amount of radiation, which in this case is infinite. A light bulb could destroy the universe! Something is off with our classical prediction.

Max Planck and the trick of quantization¶

  • In 1900 Planck found that the theoretical curve can very closely match the experimental curve if one postulates that only discrete (quantized) values of energy are possible.

Planck equation
E=hν\boxed{E= h\nu}E=hν​
(5)

  • Planck’s constant, h=6.63⋅10−34 J⋅sh = 6.63 \cdot 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}h=6.63⋅10−34J⋅s

  • Frequency ν\nuν, 1 s−11\, \text{s}^{-1}1s−1.

  • Energy EEE, 1 J1\, \text{J}1J.

  • This means atoms and molecules absorb and emit radiation in discrete quantities, multiples of hνh\nuhν, which are called quanta!

  • When light is emitted or absorbed, the atom or molecule jumps from one state to another and the energy difference hνh\nuhν is either coming from light or is used to generate light.

  • Note how small hhh is in the macroscopic units (such as J s). This is why quantization of energy is hardly noticeable and classical mechanics works so well at the macro scale. In the limit h→0h \rightarrow 0h→0, EEE becomes continuous, and an arbitrary real value of E is allowed. This is the classical limit.

The black body radiation distribution function¶

Deriving Black Body radiation formula

Planck hypothesized that the energy of oscillators in a black body is quantized and given by:

En=nhνE_n = n h \nuEn​=nhν
(6)

where nnn is a positive integer, hhh is Planck’s constant, and ν\nuν is the frequency.

The sum is given by:

Wien’s displacement law¶

  • Connecting the temperature of a black body with wavelength or frequency. The energy density peaks at a wavelength λmax\lambda_{max}λmax​ that is inversely proportional to the temperature.

  • This relationship is described by Wien’s displacement law. You can derive it by setting the derivative dρ(λ)/dλ=0d\rho(\lambda)/d\lambda=0dρ(λ)/dλ=0, which gives the wavelength at the peak of the distribution.

Wien’s displacement law
λmax=bT\lambda_{max} = \frac{b}{T}λmax​=Tb​
(22)

  • b=2.8977729⋅10−3m⋅K b=2.8977729 \cdot 10^{-3} m·Kb=2.8977729⋅10−3m⋅K.

  • This is an approximate relation connecting the temperature TTT of an object to its color, quantified by λmax\lambda_{max}λmax​.

Applications of Black Body radiation¶

Rayleigh Scattering and the Color of the Sky¶

Rayleigh Scattering

When light passes through Earth’s atmosphere, it interacts with gas molecules that are much smaller than the wavelength of visible light.

  • The scattering intensity is proportional to 1/λ41/\lambda^41/λ4, meaning shorter wavelengths (blue/violet) are scattered much more strongly than longer wavelengths (red).

  • Ultraviolet light is mostly absorbed by the ozone layer, and the human eye is less sensitive to violet than to blue.

As a result, the scattered light that fills the sky appears predominantly blue.

Problems¶

Conceptual question¶

Conceptual Question

If hotter objects radiate more strongly at shorter wavelengths, why does the daytime sky appear blue instead of violet (or even ultraviolet)?

Answer

The Sun’s spectrum indeed peaks in the green/yellow (≈500 nm), and it emits significant violet and ultraviolet.
However:

  • Rayleigh scattering in the atmosphere is much stronger at shorter wavelengths (scattering ∼1/λ4\sim 1/\lambda^4∼1/λ4).

  • Human eyes are more sensitive to blue than violet.

  • Most ultraviolet is absorbed by the ozone layer.

Thus, the scattered light that reaches us is predominantly blue.

For a monochromatic (means single wavelength) radiation with an energy of 3.5 eV3.5 \, \text{eV}3.5eV calculate the wavelength. Use Planck’s equation to relate the energy of radiation to its wavelength. The values of constants are:

Substitute the known values:

λ=(6.626×10−34 J⋅s)×(3.00×108 m/s)5.607×10−19 J\lambda = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s})}{5.607 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}}λ=5.607×10−19J(6.626×10−34J⋅s)×(3.00×108m/s)​
