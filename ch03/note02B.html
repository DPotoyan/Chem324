Applications of Particle in a Box Model#

What you will learn

  • The particle in a box (PIB) model is a simplified quantum mechanical system that can be applied to explain electronic transitions in conjugated molecules, such as those with alternating single and double bonds.

  • In these systems, the œÄ-electrons are delocalized and can be approximated as particles confined to a ‚Äúbox‚Äù formed by the length of the conjugated region.

Applications in œÄ-Conjugated Systems#

  • 1D Particle in a Box: Often used for linear conjugated systems like butadiene or polyenes. The box length corresponds to the length of the conjugated chain, and the energy levels are used to predict electronic transitions that occur when light is absorbed.

  • 2D Particle in a Box: For more complex systems like aromatic rings (e.g., benzene) or graphene fragments, the 2D PIB model helps to describe the movement of œÄ-electrons confined to two dimensions.

PIB Applied to Butadiene electronic transitions#

  • Consider the 1D PIB model applied to butadiene (C‚ÇÑH‚ÇÜ), where the four œÄ-electrons are delocalized over the entire conjugated region. Find wavelgnth of excting one pi electron from ground to the next excited state.

  • Single bond (C‚ÄìC) length: 1.54 √Ö

  • Double bond (C=C) length: 1.34 √Ö

  • In this problem, you are tasked with determining both the length of the molecule and the number of \(\pi\)-electrons in a conjugated system, specifically butadiene This requires applying the quantum particle-in-a-box (PIB) model to the system.

Step 1: Determine the Length of the Conjugated Region#

  • The conjugated \(\pi\)-electrons in butadiene are delocalized across the four carbon atoms, which form a conjugated chain. This conjugated chain can be modeled as a 1D box, with the length of the box corresponding to the length of the conjugated system.

\[ L = 1.54\, \text{√Ö} + 1.34\, \text{√Ö} + 1.54\, \text{√Ö} = 4.42\, \text{√Ö} \]

Step 2: Count the Number of \(\pi\)-Electrons#

  • In butadiene, the \(\pi\)-electrons are found in the double bonds between the carbon atoms. Each carbon in the conjugated system contributes one \(\pi\)-electron, and there are four carbons in the conjugated region. Therefore, there are 4 \(\pi\)-electrons in total.

Step 3: Apply the Particle-in-a-Box Model#

Using the 1D PIB model, the energy levels for a particle (i.e., an electron) are given by the equation:

\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \]

Where:

  • \(n\) is the quantum number (1, 2, 3,‚Ä¶),

  • \(h\) is Planck‚Äôs constant,

  • \(m\) is the mass of an electron,

  • \(L\) is the length of the conjugated region (4.42 √Ö for butadiene).

Each energy level corresponds to a state that the electron can occupy within the box.

Step 4: Calculate the Transition Energy#

When an electron absorbs energy and transitions between energy levels, the energy difference is given by:

\[ \Delta E = E_{n+1} - E_n \]

This energy difference corresponds to the energy of an absorbed photon and can be related to the wavelength of light via:

\[ \Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Where:

  • \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of the absorbed photon,

  • \(c\) is the speed of light.

By determining \(\Delta E\), you can find the corresponding wavelength of light absorbed by the molecule when an electron transitions between energy levels.

  • This approach is used to estimate the energies associated with electronic transitions in \(\pi\)-conjugated systems like butadiene, which can be experimentally observed through their absorption spectra. This also helps in understanding how molecular size and electron count affect electronic properties.

Problems#

Problem 1#

A conjugated diene molecule (e.g., butadiene) has a total conjugation length of 5 √Ö, approximated by the distance between the end carbons in the conjugated chain. Using the 1D particle in a box model, calculate the wavelength of light absorbed when a œÄ-electron is excited from the ground state (\(n = 1\)) to the first excited state (\(n = 2\)). Assume the electron behaves as a particle of mass \(m = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}\) and Planck‚Äôs constant is \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}\).

Solution 1

The energy difference between the ground state (\(n = 1\)) and the first excited state (\(n = 2\)) in a 1D particle in a box is given by:

\[ \Delta E = \frac{3h^2}{8mL^2} \]

where \(L = 5 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}\), \(m = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}\), and \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}\). First, calculate \(\Delta E\), then use the relation:

\[ \Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

to find the wavelength \(\lambda\) of the absorbed light.

Problem 2#

Consider a polyene chain of 6 alternating single and double bonds, where each C=C bond length is approximately 1.35 √Ö and each C‚ÄìC bond length is approximately 1.45 √Ö. Calculate the total conjugation length of the polyene molecule and the wavelength of light required to excite a œÄ-electron from the \(n = 1\) to \(n = 2\) state using the 1D particle in a box model.

Solution 2

First, calculate the total conjugation length by adding up the bond lengths:

\[ L = 4 \times 1.35 \, \text{√Ö} + 3 \times 1.45 \, \text{√Ö} = 10.55 \, \text{√Ö} = 10.55 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m} \]

Then, use the particle in a box energy formula to calculate \(\Delta E\) for a transition from \(n = 1\) to \(n = 2\):

\[ \Delta E = \frac{3h^2}{8mL^2} \]

Finally, find the wavelength \(\lambda\) using:

\[ \Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

Problem 3#

A linear conjugated molecule has 8 alternating single and double carbon-carbon bonds. The bond lengths are given as 1.40 √Ö for single bonds and 1.35 √Ö for double bonds. Use the particle in a box model to calculate the wavelength of light absorbed during a transition from the \(n = 1\) state to the \(n = 3\) state.

Solution 3

Calculate the total length of the conjugated chain by adding the single and double bond lengths. For an 8-bond system, you have 4 double bonds and 4 single bonds, so:

\[ L = 4 \times 1.35 \, \text{√Ö} + 4 \times 1.40 \, \text{√Ö} = 10.0 \, \text{√Ö} = 10.0 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m} \]

Then, calculate the energy difference for the transition from \(n = 1\) to \(n = 3\):

\[ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = \frac{9h^2}{8mL^2} - \frac{h^2}{8mL^2} = \frac{8h^2}{8mL^2} = \frac{h^2}{mL^2} \]

Finally, find \(\lambda\) using \(\Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}\).

Problem 4#

A conjugated triene molecule has a total conjugation length of 7.5 √Ö, derived from three alternating single and double carbon-carbon bonds. Calculate the wavelength of light absorbed when a œÄ-electron transitions from the ground state (\(n = 1\)) to the second excited state (\(n = 3\)) in the 1D particle in a box model. Use the same constants as above.

Solution 4

Using \(L = 7.5 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}\), calculate the energy difference between \(n = 1\) and \(n = 3\):

\[ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = \frac{9h^2}{8mL^2} - \frac{h^2}{8mL^2} = \frac{8h^2}{8mL^2} \]

Use this to find the wavelength of the absorbed light.

Problem 5#

A conjugated polyene chain consists of 10 carbon atoms connected by alternating single and double bonds. Each C=C bond is 1.34 √Ö, and each C‚ÄìC bond is 1.54 √Ö. Find the total length of the conjugated system, and use this to calculate the wavelength of the transition from the \(n = 2\) to the \(n = 3\) energy level. Assume all œÄ-electrons are delocalized over the entire length of the molecule.

Solution 5

Calculate the total length of the polyene:

\[ L = 5 \times 1.34 \, \text{√Ö} + 4 \times 1.54 \, \text{√Ö} = 12.86 \, \text{√Ö} = 12.86 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m} \]

Then, calculate \(\Delta E\) for the \(n = 2\) to \(n = 3\) transition:

\[ \Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = \frac{9h^2}{8mL^2} - \frac{4h^2}{8mL^2} = \frac{5h^2}{8mL^2} \]

Finally, use the relation \(\Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}\) to calculate the wavelength.

Problem 6#

Consider an electron in a 2D particle in a box, representing the œÄ-electrons in a graphene-like fragment with dimensions \(L_x = 2 \, \text{nm}\) and \(L_y = 1 \, \text{nm}\). Calculate the energy of the electron for the quantum numbers \(n_x = 1\) and \(n_y = 2\)

Solution 6

The energy for a 2D particle in a box is given by:

\[ E_{n_x,n_y} = \frac{h^2}{8m} \left( \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} \right) \]

Using \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}{J \cdot s}\) \(\,\), \(m = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}\), \(L_x = 2 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}\), and \(L_y = 1 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}\), we calculate:

\[ E_{1,2} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})^2}{8 \times 9.109 \times 10^{-31}} \left( \frac{1^2}{(2 \times 10^{-9})^2} + \frac{2^2}{(1 \times 10^{-9})^2} \right) \]

First, compute each term:

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1^2}{(2 \times 10^{-9})^2} = \frac{1}{4 \times 10^{-18}} = 2.5 \times 10^{17}\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{2^2}{(1 \times 10^{-9})^2} = \frac{4}{1 \times 10^{-18}} = 4.0 \times 10^{18}\)

So,

\[ \left( \frac{1^2}{(2 \times 10^{-9})^2} + \frac{2^2}{(1 \times 10^{-9})^2} \right) = 2.5 \times 10^{17} + 4.0 \times 10^{18} = 4.25 \times 10^{18} \]

Now calculate the prefactor:

\[ \frac{h^2}{8m} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})^2}{8 \times 9.109 \times 10^{-31}} = \frac{4.39 \times 10^{-67}}{7.287 \times 10^{-30}} \approx 6.02 \times 10^{-38} \]

Therefore,

\[ E_{1,2} \approx (6.02 \times 10^{-38})(4.25 \times 10^{18}) = 2.56 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \]

Convert to electronvolts:

\[ E_{1,2} = \frac{2.56 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 1.60 \, \text{eV} \]

The energy of the electron in the 2D box for \(n_x = 1\) and \(n_y = 2\) is:

\[ E_{1,2} \approx 2.56 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \quad \text{or} \quad 1.60 \, \text{eV}. \]
