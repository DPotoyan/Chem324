7. Orbital overlap¶

Bonding starts here. The overlap integral S=∫ψAψB d3rS = \int \psi_A \psi_B\,d^3rS=∫ψA​ψB​d3r measures how much two atomic orbitals share the same region with the same sign. Pick an orbital on each center, slide them together, and watch the overlap. The left panel shows both orbitals along the bond (zzz) axis; the right panel is the product ψAψB\psi_A\psi_BψA​ψB​ whose integral is SSS. Try 1s with 2p_x to see an overlap that cancels to zero by symmetry.

The window where ∣S∣|S|∣S∣ is a few tenths is exactly where chemical bonds live. Pushing the nuclei together raises ∣S∣|S|∣S∣, while a mismatch in orbital symmetry drives it to zero no matter how close they get.

8. One-dimensional Schrödinger solver¶

Pick a potential and get its bound states instantly: energies as horizontal lines, wavefunctions drawn at their own energy (units: ℏ=m=1\hbar = m = 1ℏ=m=1, hard walls at x=±4x = \pm 4x=±4). To see how the solver works inside, open the numerical Schrödinger lab.

Try the classics: the box gives the n2n^2n2 ladder, the harmonic well gives perfectly even spacing (the fingerprint of vibrations), the linear well spaces levels like Airy zeros, and the double well pairs levels into tunneling doublets.
