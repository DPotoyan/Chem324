Huckel Molecular Orbital Theory

What you will learn

  • Conjugated and aromatic systems with delocalized π\piπ electrons are poorly described by localized valence bond pictures and call for a dedicated approach.

  • Huckel theory treats the π\piπ electrons independently and reduces the secular problem to a matrix built from just two empirical parameters: the Coulomb integral α\alphaα and the resonance integral β\betaβ.

  • Diagonalizing the Huckel matrix gives the π\piπ orbital energies and coefficients, and the energy gap predicts the lowest electronic excitation.

  • Worked examples for ethylene, 1,3-butadiene, and benzene show how delocalization lowers the total π\piπ energy and produces the aromatic stabilization of benzene.

Conjugated systems¶

  • Molecules with extensive π\piπ bonding systems, such as benzene, are not described very well by valence bond theory because the π\piπ electrons are delocalized over the whole molecule. The σ\sigmaσ and π\piπ bonds are illustrated below for ethylene (C2H4C_2H_4C2​H4​, with sp2sp^2sp2 carbons):

  • We have chosen the zzz-axis along the internuclear axis. Because both σ\sigmaσ and π\piπ bonding occur between the two carbon atoms, we say that this is a double bond. The hybrid orbitals here also explain the geometry. For triple bonds, one σ\sigmaσ and two π\piπ bonds are formed.

Huckel MO theory¶

  • Huckel molecular orbital theory assumes that the π\piπ electrons, which are responsible for the special properties of conjugated and aromatic hydrocarbons, do not interact with one another, and that the total wavefunction is just a product of the one-electron molecular orbitals. The π\piπ molecular orbital of the two carbons in C2H4C_2H_4C2​H4​ can be written approximately as:

ψ=c1ϕ1+c2ϕ2{\psi = c_1\phi_1 + c_2\phi_2}ψ=c1​ϕ1​+c2​ϕ2​
(1)

  • where ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​ and ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​ are the 2py2p_y2py​ atomic orbitals for carbons 1 and 2, respectively. Using the variational principle gives the following secular determinant:

∣H11−ES11H12−ES12H21−ES21H22−ES22∣=0 with Hij=∫ϕi∗Hϕjdτ and Sij=∫ϕi∗ϕjdτ{\begin{vmatrix} H_{11} - ES_{11} & H_{12} - ES_{12}\\ H_{21} - ES_{21} & H_{22} - ES_{22}\\ \end{vmatrix} = 0 \textnormal{ with } H_{ij} = \int\phi_i^*H\phi_jd\tau \textnormal{ and } S_{ij} = \int\phi_i^*\phi_jd\tau}∣∣​H11​−ES11​H21​−ES21​​H12​−ES12​H22​−ES22​​∣∣​=0 with Hij​=∫ϕi∗​Hϕj​dτ and Sij​=∫ϕi∗​ϕj​dτ
(2)
The Huckel approximations

In Huckel theory, the secular equation is simplified by assuming:

  1. All overlap integrals SijS_{ij}Sij​ are set to zero unless i=ji = ji=j, when Sii=1S_{ii} = 1Sii​=1.

  2. All diagonal matrix elements HiiH_{ii}Hii​ are set to a constant denoted α\alphaα.

  3. The resonance integrals HijH_{ij}Hij​ (i≠ji \ne ji=j) are set to zero except for those on neighboring atoms, which are set equal to a constant β\betaβ. The indices here also identify atoms, because the atomic orbitals are centered on atoms.

With these rules, the secular determinant for ethylene becomes:

∣α−Eββα−E∣=0{\begin{vmatrix}\alpha - E & \beta\\ \beta & \alpha - E\\ \end{vmatrix} = 0}∣∣​α−Eβ​βα−E​∣∣​=0
(3)

  • In Huckel theory, the Coulomb integral α\alphaα and the resonance integral β\betaβ are regarded as empirical parameters. They can be obtained, for example, from experimental data. Thus, in Huckel theory it is not necessary to specify the Hamiltonian operator. Expansion of the determinant leads to a quadratic equation for EEE, with solutions E=α±βE = \alpha \pm \betaE=α±β. In general, it can be shown that β<0\beta < 0β<0, which implies that the lowest orbital energy is E1=α+βE_1 = \alpha + \betaE1​=α+β. There are two π\piπ electrons, and therefore the total energy is Etot=2E1=2α+2βE_{tot} = 2E_1 = 2\alpha + 2\betaEtot​=2E1​=2α+2β. Do not confuse α\alphaα and β\betaβ here with electron spin.

Solving for the Huckel MOs¶

The wavefunctions (the coefficients c1c_1c1​ and c2c_2c2​) can be obtained by substituting the two values of EEE into the original linear equations:

c1(α−E)+c2β=0{c_1(\alpha - E) + c_2\beta = 0}c1​(α−E)+c2​β=0
(4)
c1β+c2(α−E)=0{c_1\beta + c_2(\alpha - E) = 0}c1​β+c2​(α−E)=0
(5)

For the lowest energy orbital (E1=α+βE_1 = \alpha + \betaE1​=α+β), we get (including normalization):

ψ1=12(ϕ1+ϕ2)(i.e. c1=c2=12){\psi_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\phi_1 + \phi_2) \hspace{0.5cm}(\textnormal{i.e. }c_1 = c_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})}ψ1​=2​1​(ϕ1​+ϕ2​)(i.e. c1​=c2​=2​1​)
(6)

and for the highest energy orbital (E2=α−βE_2 = \alpha - \betaE2​=α−β) (including normalization):

ψ2=12(ϕ1−ϕ2)(i.e. c1=12,c2=−12){\psi_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\phi_1 - \phi_2) \hspace{0.5cm}(\textnormal{i.e. }c_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, c_2 = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})}ψ2​=2​1​(ϕ1​−ϕ2​)(i.e. c1​=2​1​,c2​=−2​1​)
(7)

These orbitals resemble the H2+H_2^+H2+​ LCAO MOs discussed previously. This also gives us an estimate for one of the excited states, where one electron is promoted from the bonding to the antibonding orbital. The excitation energy is found to be 2∣β∣2|\beta|2∣β∣, which allows, for example, the estimation of β\betaβ from UV/VIS absorption spectroscopy.

Example: 1,3-butadiene¶

  • Let us calculate the π\piπ electronic energy for 1,3-butadiene (CH2=CHCH=CH2CH_2=CHCH=CH_2CH2​=CHCH=CH2​) using Huckel theory. First we write the secular determinant using the rules given earlier. To do this, it is convenient to number the carbon atoms in the molecule:

CH21=CH2−CH3=CH24\overset{1}{\textnormal{CH}_2} = \overset{2}{\textnormal{CH}} - \overset{3}{\textnormal{CH}} = \overset{4}{\textnormal{CH}_2}CH2​1​=CH2−CH3=CH2​4​
(8)

In this case there are two scenarios to consider:

  1. A localized solution where the π\piπ electrons are shared either between atoms 1 and 2 or between atoms 3 and 4. This implies that the β\betaβ parameter should not be written between nuclei 2 and 3.

  2. A delocalized solution where the π\piπ electrons are delocalized over all four carbons. This implies that the β\betaβ parameter should be written between nuclei 2 and 3.

Here it turns out that scenario 2 gives a lower energy solution, and we study that in more detail. In general, however, both cases should be considered. The energy difference between scenarios 1 and 2 is called the resonance stabilization energy. The secular determinant is:

1234∣α−Eβ00βα−Eβ00βα−Eβ00βα−E∣=0{\begin{matrix} 1\\2\\3\\4\\ \end{matrix}\begin{vmatrix} \alpha - E & \beta & 0 & 0\\ \beta & \alpha - E & \beta & 0\\ 0 & \beta & \alpha - E & \beta\\ 0 & 0 & \beta & \alpha - E\\ \end{vmatrix} = 0}1234​∣∣​α−Eβ00​βα−Eβ0​0βα−Eβ​00βα−E​∣∣​=0
(9)

To simplify the notation, we divide each row by β\betaβ and denote x=(α−E)/βx = (\alpha - E) / \betax=(α−E)/β:

∣x1001x1001x1001x∣=0{\begin{vmatrix} x & 1 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & x & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & x & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & x\\ \end{vmatrix} = 0}∣∣​x100​1x10​01x1​001x​∣∣​=0
(10)

Expansion of this determinant gives x4−3x2+1=0x^4 - 3x^2 + 1 = 0x4−3x2+1=0. There are four solutions, x=±0.618x = \pm 0.618x=±0.618 and x=±1.618x = \pm 1.618x=±1.618. Thus there are four possible orbital energy levels:

E1=α+1.618β(lowest energy)E2=α+0.618βE3=α−0.618βE4=α−1.618β(highest energy){E_1 = \alpha + 1.618\beta \hspace{0.5cm}\textnormal{(lowest energy)}} \\ {E_2 = \alpha + 0.618\beta} \\ {E_3 = \alpha - 0.618\beta} \\ {E_4 = \alpha - 1.618\beta \hspace{0.5cm}\textnormal{(highest energy)}}E1​=α+1.618β(lowest energy)E2​=α+0.618βE3​=α−0.618βE4​=α−1.618β(highest energy)
(11)

There are four π\piπ electrons, which occupy the two lowest energy orbitals. This gives the total π\piπ electronic energy of the molecule:

Eπ=2(α+1.618β)+2(α+0.618β)=4α+4.472β{E_\pi = 2(\alpha + 1.618\beta) + 2(\alpha + 0.618\beta) = 4\alpha + 4.472\beta}Eπ​=2(α+1.618β)+2(α+0.618β)=4α+4.472β
(12)

and the lowest excitation energy is 1.236∣β∣1.236|\beta|1.236∣β∣.

(13)
ψ2=0.602ϕ1+0.372ϕ2−0.372ϕ3−0.602ϕ4{\psi_2 = 0.602\phi_1 + 0.372\phi_2 - 0.372\phi_3 - 0.602\phi_4}ψ2​=0.602ϕ1​+0.372ϕ2​−0.372ϕ3​−0.602ϕ4​
(14)
ψ3=0.602ϕ1−0.372ϕ2−0.372ϕ3+0.602ϕ4{\psi_3 = 0.602\phi_1 - 0.372\phi_2 - 0.372\phi_3 + 0.602\phi_4}ψ3​=0.602ϕ1​−0.372ϕ2​−0.372ϕ3​+0.602ϕ4​
(15)
ψ4=0.372ϕ1−0.602ϕ2+0.602ϕ3−0.372ϕ4{\psi_4 = 0.372\phi_1 - 0.602\phi_2 + 0.602\phi_3 - 0.372\phi_4}ψ4​=0.372ϕ1​−0.602ϕ2​+0.602ϕ3​−0.372ϕ4​

Example: the benzene molecule¶

  • Let us now apply the Huckel method to the benzene molecule. The secular determinant for benzene is (electrons delocalized):

∣α−Eβ000ββα−Eβ0000βα−Eβ0000βα−Eβ0000βα−Eββ000βα−E∣=0{ \begin{vmatrix} \alpha - E & \beta & 0 & 0 & 0 & \beta\\ \beta & \alpha - E & \beta & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & \beta & \alpha - E & \beta & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \beta & \alpha - E & \beta & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \beta & \alpha - E & \beta\\ \beta & 0 & 0 & 0 & \beta & \alpha - E\\ \end{vmatrix} = 0 }∣∣​α−Eβ000β​βα−Eβ000​0βα−Eβ00​00βα−Eβ0​000βα−Eβ​β000βα−E​∣∣​=0
(17)

The solutions are (showing where the six π\piπ electrons should be placed):

E1=α+2β  (lowest energy)E2=E3=α+βE4=E5=α−βE6=α−2β  (highest energy){E_1 = \alpha + 2\beta~~\textnormal{(lowest energy)}} \\ {E_2 = E_3 = \alpha + \beta} \\ {E_4 = E_5 = \alpha - \beta} \\ {E_6 = \alpha - 2\beta~~\textnormal{(highest energy)}}E1​=α+2β  (lowest energy)E2​=E3​=α+βE4​=E5​=α−βE6​=α−2β  (highest energy)
(18)

The six π\piπ electrons fill the lowest three levels (E1E_1E1​ and the degenerate pair E2=E3E_2 = E_3E2​=E3​), giving a total π\piπ energy of 6α+8β6\alpha + 8\beta6α+8β. Compared with three isolated ethylene double bonds (6α+6β6\alpha + 6\beta6α+6β), benzene is stabilized by an extra 2β2\beta2β, which is the origin of its aromatic stability.
