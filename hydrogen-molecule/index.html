Molecular Orbital Description of the Hydrogen Molecule

What you will learn

  • The neutral H2H_2H2​ molecule has two electrons, which introduces an electron-electron repulsion term 1/r121/r_{12}1/r12​ that makes the electronic Schrodinger equation analytically unsolvable.

  • We build the ground state of H2H_2H2​ by placing two electrons of opposite spin in the bonding 1σg1\sigma_g1σg​ orbital, writing the total wavefunction as an antisymmetric Slater determinant.

  • The simple LCAO-MO wavefunction already predicts a bound molecule, though it overestimates the bond length and underestimates the binding energy.

  • Accuracy is systematically improved by separating ionic and covalent contributions and ultimately by configuration interaction, which reaches essentially exact agreement with experiment.

Setting up the Hamiltonian¶

(1)

  • The main difficulty in the molecular Hamiltonian is the 1/r121/r_{12}1/r12​ term, which couples the two electrons to each other. This means that a simple product wavefunction is not sufficient. No analytic solution has been found for the electronic Schrodinger equation of H2H_2H2​.

  • For this reason, we solve the problem approximately by using the LCAO-MO approach used previously. For example, the ground state for H2H_2H2​ is obtained by placing two electrons with opposite spins in the 1σg1\sigma_g1σg​ orbital. This assumes that the wavefunction is expressed as an antisymmetrized product (a Slater determinant).

Constructing MOs for the hydrogen molecule¶

  • According to the Pauli principle, two electrons with opposite spins can be assigned to a given spatial orbital. As a first approximation, we assume that the molecular orbitals in H2H_2H2​ remain the same as in H2+H_2^+H2+​. Hence both electrons occupy the 1σg1\sigma_g1σg​ orbital (the ground state) and the electronic configuration is denoted (1σg)21\sigma_g)^21σg​)2. This is similar to the notation used previously for atoms (for example, the He atom is (1s)21s)^21s)2).

  • The molecular orbital for electron 1 in the 1σg1\sigma_g1σg​ molecular orbital is:

1σg(1)=12(1+S)(1sA(1)+1sB(1)){1\sigma_g(1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(1 + S)}}(1s_A(1) + 1s_B(1))}1σg​(1)=2(1+S)​1​(1sA​(1)+1sB​(1))
(2)

  • Previously we found that the total wavefunction must be antisymmetric with respect to exchange of electron indices. This can be achieved by using the Slater determinant:

  • Note that this wavefunction is only approximate and is definitely not an eigenfunction of the H2H_2H2​ electronic Hamiltonian. Thus we must calculate the electronic energy by taking the expectation value of this wavefunction with the Hamiltonian (the actual calculation is not shown):

W. Atkins, Molecular Quantum Mechanics, Oxford University Press). With this approach, the minimum energy is reached at RRR = 84 pm (experimental 74.1 pm) with a dissociation energy DeD_eDe​ = 255 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1 (experimental 458 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1).

Improving upon the simple MO approximation¶

  • This simple approach is not very accurate, but it demonstrates that the method works. To improve the accuracy, ionic and covalent terms should be considered separately:

  • Note that the variational constants c1c_1c1​ and c2c_2c2​ depend on the internuclear distance RRR. Minimization of the energy expectation value with respect to these constants gives ReR_eRe​ = 74.9 pm (experiment 74.1 pm) and DeD_eDe​ = 386 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1 (experiment 458 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1).

  • Further improvement can be achieved by adding higher atomic orbitals to the wavefunction. The previously discussed Hartree-Fock method provides an efficient way of solving the problem. Recall that this method is only approximate, as it ignores electron-electron correlation effects completely. The full treatment requires configuration interaction methods, which can yield essentially exact results: DeD_eDe​ = 36117.8 cm−1^{-1}−1 (CI) versus 36117.3±1.036117.3\pm1.036117.3±1.0 cm−1^{-1}−1 (experiment), and ReR_eRe​ = 74.140 pm versus 74.139 pm (experiment).
