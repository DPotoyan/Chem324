The Hydrogen Molecule Ion

What you will learn

  • We can construct an approximate trial wavefunction for a molecule using atomic orbitals to gain intuition about chemical bonding.

  • Applying the variational method leads to two molecular orbitals (MOs) with distinct spatial character: one increases the electron probability density between the nuclei (bonding), while the other decreases it (antibonding).

  • These bonding and antibonding MOs respectively lower and raise the energy of the molecule relative to two separated atoms.

  • More accurate quantum chemical methods do not rely directly on atomic orbitals, but instead use abstract linear combinations of Gaussian basis sets to represent molecular orbitals.

Constructing MOs from AOs¶

The electronic Schrodinger equation for H2+H_2^+H2+​ can be solved exactly because the system contains only a single electron. However, the analytical solution involves quite challenging mathematics. Instead, we adopt a simpler approximate approach that still captures the essential physics of chemical bonding.

We construct a trial (approximate) molecular wavefunction as a real linear combination of hydrogen 1s atomic orbitals:

ψ±(r⃗1)=c1 1sA(r⃗1)  ±  c2 1sB(r⃗1)\psi_{\pm}(\vec{r}_1) = c_1\,1s_A(\vec{r}_1) \; \pm \; c_2\,1s_B(\vec{r}_1)ψ±​(r1​)=c1​1sA​(r1​)±c2​1sB​(r1​)
(1)

  • Here, 1sA1s_A1sA​ and 1sB1s_B1sB​ are hydrogen 1s orbitals centered on nuclei A and B, respectively, and c1c_1c1​ and c2c_2c2​ are coefficients (constants).

  • This form is known as the Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO) approximation for constructing molecular orbitals.

  • Because the two nuclei are identical, symmetry requires c1=c2≡cc_1 = c_2 \equiv cc1​=c2​≡c (and we choose c>0c > 0c>0).

  • The ± sign indicates that two distinct molecular orbitals can be formed:

    • the bonding combination (+++), which enhances electron density between the nuclei

    • the antibonding combination (−-−), which decreases electron density between the nuclei

  • Normalization of the molecular wavefunction requires:

∫ψ±∗ψ±dτ=1{\int{\psi_\pm^*\psi_\pm d\tau} = 1}∫ψ±∗​ψ±​dτ=1
(2)

Bonding versus antibonding orbitals¶

  • The main feature of a chemical bond is the increased electron density between the nuclei. This identifies the +++ wavefunction as a bonding orbital and the −-− wavefunction as an antibonding orbital.

  • When a molecule has a center of symmetry (here halfway between the nuclei), the wavefunction may or may not change sign when it is inverted through the center of symmetry. If the origin is placed at the center of symmetry, then we can assign symmetry labels ggg and uuu to the wavefunctions.

  • If ψ(x,y,z)=ψ(−x,−y,−z)\psi(x, y, z) = \psi(-x, -y, -z)ψ(x,y,z)=ψ(−x,−y,−z) then the symmetry label is ggg (even parity), and for ψ(x,y,z)=−ψ(−x,−y,−z)\psi(x, y, z) = -\psi(-x, -y, -z)ψ(x,y,z)=−ψ(−x,−y,−z) we have the uuu label (odd parity).

  • According to this notation, the ggg symmetry orbital is the bonding orbital and the uuu symmetry corresponds to the antibonding orbital. Later we will see that this is reversed for π\piπ-orbitals.

  • The overlap integral S(R)S(R)S(R) can be evaluated analytically (derivation not shown):

S(R)=e−R(1+R+R33){S(R) = e^{-R}\left( 1 + R + \frac{R^3}{3}\right)}S(R)=e−R(1+R+3R3​)
(10)

  • Note that when R=0R = 0R=0 (the nuclei overlap), S(0)=1S(0) = 1S(0)=1, which is a useful check that the expression is reasonable.

Energy of the hydrogen molecule ion¶

  • Using a linear combination of atomic orbitals, it is possible to calculate the best values, in terms of energy, of the coefficients c1c_1c1​ and c2c_2c2​. Remember that this linear combination can only provide an approximate solution to the H2+H_2^+H2+​ Schrodinger equation. The variational principle provides a systematic way to calculate the energy when RRR (the distance between the nuclei) is fixed:

(11)
=c12HAA+2c1c2HAB+c22HBBc12SAA+2c1c2SAB+c22SBB{= \frac{c_1^2H_{AA} + 2c_1c_2H_{AB} + c_2^2H_{BB}} {c_1^2 S_{AA} + 2c_1c_2 S_{AB} + c_2^2 S_{BB}}}=c12​SAA​+2c1​c2​SAB​+c22​SBB​c12​HAA​+2c1​c2​HAB​+c22​HBB​​

where SAA=SBB=1S_{AA} = S_{BB} = 1SAA​=SBB​=1 and SAB=SS_{AB} = SSAB​=S.

  • Here HAAH_{AA}HAA​, HABH_{AB}HAB​, HBBH_{BB}HBB​, SAAS_{AA}SAA​, SABS_{AB}SAB​, and SBBS_{BB}SBB​ denote the integrals occurring in the variational treatment of the problem.   • To minimize the energy expectation value with respect to c1c_1c1​ and c2c_2c2​, we calculate the partial derivatives of the energy with respect to these parameters:

Comparison of MO energies with experiment¶

  • These values can be compared with experimental results. The calculated ground state equilibrium bond length is 132 pm, whereas the experimental value is 106 pm. The binding energy is 170 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1, whereas the experimental value is 258 kJ mol−1^{-1}−1.

  • The excited state (labeled with uuu) leads to repulsive behavior at all bond lengths RRR (it is antibonding). Because the uuu state lies higher in energy than the ggg state, the uuu state is an excited state of H2+H_2^+H2+​.

  • This calculation can be made more accurate by adding more than two terms to the linear combination. This procedure would also yield more excited state solutions. These would correspond to uuu/ggg combinations of 2s2s2s, 2px2p_x2px​, 2py2p_y2py​, 2pz2p_z2pz​, and so on.

MO diagrams¶

  • It is common practice to represent the molecular orbitals using molecular orbital (MO) diagrams. The formation of bonding and antibonding orbitals can be visualized as follows:

  • σ\sigmaσ orbitals. When two sss or pzp_zpz​ orbitals interact, a σ\sigmaσ molecular orbital is formed. The notation σ\sigmaσ specifies the amount of angular momentum about the molecular axis (for σ\sigmaσ, λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0 with Lz=±λℏL_z = \pm\lambda\hbarLz​=±λℏ). In many-electron systems, both bonding and antibonding σ\sigmaσ orbitals can each hold a maximum of two electrons. Antibonding orbitals are often denoted by an asterisk.

  • π\piπ orbitals. When two px,yp_{x,y}px,y​ orbitals interact, a π\piπ molecular orbital forms. π\piπ-orbitals are doubly degenerate: π+1\pi_{+1}π+1​ and π−1\pi_{-1}π−1​ (or alternatively πx\pi_xπx​ and πy\pi_yπy​), where the +1/−1+1/-1+1/−1 refer to the eigenvalue of the LzL_zLz​ operator (λ=±1\lambda = \pm1λ=±1). In many-electron systems a bonding π\piπ-orbital can therefore hold a maximum of 4 electrons (both π+1\pi_{+1}π+1​ and π−1\pi_{-1}π−1​ each hold two electrons). The same holds for the antibonding π\piπ orbitals. Note that only atomic orbitals of the same symmetry mix to form molecular orbitals (for example, pz−pzp_z - p_zpz​−pz​, px−pxp_x - p_xpx​−px​, and py−pyp_y - p_ypy​−py​). When atomic ddd orbitals mix to form molecular orbitals, σ\sigmaσ (λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0), π\piπ (λ=±1\lambda = \pm 1λ=±1), and δ\deltaδ (λ=±2\lambda = \pm 2λ=±2) MOs form.
