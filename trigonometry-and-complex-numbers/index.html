Trigonometry and Complex Numbers

What you will learn

  • Trigonometry as geometry on the unit circle. Sine and cosine are the coordinates of a point rotating around a circle of radius one.

  • The key identities. The Pythagorean identity, angle-sum formulas, and power-reduction formulas that recur throughout quantum mechanics.

  • Complex numbers as 2D numbers. A complex number carries a real and an imaginary part and lives in a plane.

  • Euler’s formula. eiϕ=cos⁡ϕ+isin⁡ϕe^{i\phi} = \cos\phi + i\sin\phieiϕ=cosϕ+isinϕ fuses trigonometry and exponentials into one object and is the single most useful identity in the course.

  • Polar form, conjugation, and rotation. Multiplying complex numbers rotates and scales them, and the conjugate reflects across the real axis.

Trigonometry and complex numbers are two views of the same thing: rotation in a plane. Waves, oscillations, and quantum phases all live here, and Euler’s formula is the bridge that connects them. We treat them together because in quantum mechanics you almost never use one without the other.

Trigonometry¶

Sine and cosine on the unit circle¶

Take a point on a circle of radius 1 and rotate it counterclockwise by an angle θ\thetaθ (measured in radians, where a full turn is 2π2\pi2π). Its coordinates are, by definition,

x=cos⁡θ,y=sin⁡θ.x = \cos\theta, \qquad y = \sin\theta.x=cosθ,y=sinθ.
(1)

Cosine is the horizontal coordinate, sine the vertical one, and the tangent tan⁡θ=sin⁡θ/cos⁡θ\tan\theta = \sin\theta/\cos\thetatanθ=sinθ/cosθ is the slope of the radius.

Key identities¶

Because the point lies on a unit circle, its coordinates always satisfy the Pythagorean identity:

Pythagorean identity
cos⁡2θ+sin⁡2θ=1\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1cos2θ+sin2θ=1
(2)

The identities you will reach for most often are the angle-sum and power-reduction formulas:

sin⁡(A±B)=sin⁡Acos⁡B±cos⁡Asin⁡B,cos⁡(A±B)=cos⁡Acos⁡B∓sin⁡Asin⁡B,\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A\cos B \pm \cos A\sin B, \qquad \cos(A \pm B) = \cos A\cos B \mp \sin A\sin B,sin(A±B)=sinAcosB±cosAsinB,cos(A±B)=cosAcosB∓sinAsinB,
(3)
sin⁡2θ=12(1−cos⁡2θ),cos⁡2θ=12(1+cos⁡2θ).\sin^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-\cos 2\theta), \qquad \cos^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\cos 2\theta).sin2θ=21​(1−cos2θ),cos2θ=21​(1+cos2θ).
(4)

For small angles (in radians), the first terms of the Taylor series give the approximations sin⁡θ≈θ\sin\theta \approx \thetasinθ≈θ and cos⁡θ≈1−12θ2\cos\theta \approx 1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2cosθ≈1−21​θ2, which are what let us linearize a pendulum or a vibrating bond near equilibrium.

Rather than memorize the whole table of identities, it is easier to derive them from Euler’s formula, which we build next.

Complex numbers¶

Complex numbers live in 2D¶

A complex number zzz is a two-dimensional number: it needs two components for its full specification. They arise the moment we ask for the roots of a simple polynomial. The equation

x2+1=0⟹x1,2=±−1=±ix^2 + 1 = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x_{1,2} = \pm\sqrt{-1} = \pm ix2+1=0⟹x1,2​=±−1​=±i
(5)

has no real solution, so we extend the real line R\mathbb{R}R into the complex plane C\mathbb{C}C by introducing the imaginary unit iii, defined solely by

i2=−1.i^2 = -1.i2=−1.
(6)
The quadratic formula and the discriminant

For ax2+bx+c=0ax^2 + bx + c = 0ax2+bx+c=0 the roots are x=−b±b2−4ac2ax = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}x=2a−b±b2−4ac​​. The discriminant Δ=b2−4ac\Delta = b^2 - 4acΔ=b2−4ac decides their nature: Δ>0\Delta > 0Δ>0 gives two real roots, Δ=0\Delta = 0Δ=0 a repeated real root, and Δ<0\Delta < 0Δ<0 two complex conjugate roots.

Cartesian representation¶

Cartesian form
z=x+iyz = x + iyz=x+iy
(7)

  • x=Re⁡(z)x = \operatorname{Re}(z)x=Re(z) is the real part,

  • y=Im⁡(z)y = \operatorname{Im}(z)y=Im(z) is the imaginary part.

Polar representation and Euler’s formula¶

Instead of horizontal and vertical parts, we can locate zzz by its distance from the origin rrr and its angle ϕ\phiϕ. From the unit-circle picture, x=rcos⁡ϕx = r\cos\phix=rcosϕ and y=rsin⁡ϕy = r\sin\phiy=rsinϕ, so

z=x+iy=r(cos⁡ϕ+isin⁡ϕ).z = x + iy = r(\cos\phi + i\sin\phi).z=x+iy=r(cosϕ+isinϕ).
(8)

The magic step is Euler’s formula, which collapses the parenthesis into an exponential:

Euler’s formula
eiϕ=cos⁡ϕ+isin⁡ϕ⟹z=r eiϕe^{i\phi} = \cos\phi + i\sin\phi \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad z = r\,e^{i\phi}eiϕ=cosϕ+isinϕ⟹z=reiϕ
(9)

Here r=x2+y2r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}r=x2+y2​ is the magnitude and ϕ\phiϕ is the phase angle.

Proof of Euler’s formula from Taylor series

Expand eixe^{ix}eix and collect powers of iii:

eix=1+ix−x22!−ix33!+x44!+⋯=(1−x22!+⋯ )+i(x−x33!+⋯ ).e^{ix} = 1 + ix - \frac{x^2}{2!} - i\frac{x^3}{3!} + \frac{x^4}{4!} + \cdots = \left(1 - \frac{x^2}{2!} + \cdots\right) + i\left(x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots\right).eix=1+ix−2!x2​−i3!x3​+4!x4​+⋯=(1−2!x2​+⋯)+i(x−3!x3​+⋯).
(10)

The two series are exactly cos⁡x\cos xcosx and sin⁡x\sin xsinx, so eix=cos⁡x+isin⁡xe^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin xeix=cosx+isinx.

Example: Convert to polar form

Write z=−1−2iz = -1 - 2iz=−1−2i in polar form. Use arctan2, which picks the correct quadrant.

r=(−1)2+(−2)2=5,ϕ=arctan2⁡(−2,−1)≈−2.034.r = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{5}, \qquad \phi = \operatorname{arctan2}(-2, -1) \approx -2.034.r=(−1)2+(−2)2​=5​,ϕ=arctan2(−2,−1)≈−2.034.
(11)

Adding 2π2\pi2π gives an equivalent positive angle ϕ≈4.248\phi \approx 4.248ϕ≈4.248, so z=5 e4.248 iz = \sqrt{5}\,e^{4.248\,i}z=5​e4.248i.

Multiplication is rotation¶

In polar form, multiplication becomes almost trivial: magnitudes multiply and angles add.

(r1eiϕ1)(r2eiϕ2)=r1r2 ei(ϕ1+ϕ2).(r_1 e^{i\phi_1})(r_2 e^{i\phi_2}) = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\phi_1 + \phi_2)}.(r1​eiϕ1​)(r2​eiϕ2​)=r1​r2​ei(ϕ1​+ϕ2​).
(12)

So multiplying by eiϕe^{i\phi}eiϕ rotates a number counterclockwise by ϕ\phiϕ without changing its length. Multiplying by i=eiπ/2i = e^{i\pi/2}i=eiπ/2 is a quarter-turn. This is the same rotation that trigonometry describes, now in one clean line of algebra.

The complex conjugate¶

The conjugate flips the sign of the imaginary part, reflecting zzz across the real axis:

Complex conjugate
zˉ=x−iy=r e−iϕ\bar{z} = x - iy = r\,e^{-i\phi}zˉ=x−iy=re−iϕ
(13)

Multiplying a number by its conjugate rotates it back onto the real axis and returns a non-negative real number, the squared magnitude:

∣z∣2=zˉ z=(x−iy)(x+iy)=x2+y2=r2.|z|^2 = \bar{z}\,z = (x - iy)(x + iy) = x^2 + y^2 = r^2.∣z∣2=zˉz=(x−iy)(x+iy)=x2+y2=r2.
(14)

This is exactly the operation that turns a quantum amplitude ψ\psiψ into a probability density ∣ψ∣2=ψ∗ψ|\psi|^2 = \psi^*\psi∣ψ∣2=ψ∗ψ.

Taking sums and differences of zzz and zˉ\bar{z}zˉ recovers the trig functions as exponentials, a substitution that simplifies countless integrals:

cos⁡ϕ=eiϕ+e−iϕ2,sin⁡ϕ=eiϕ−e−iϕ2i.\cos\phi = \frac{e^{i\phi} + e^{-i\phi}}{2}, \qquad \sin\phi = \frac{e^{i\phi} - e^{-i\phi}}{2i}.cosϕ=2eiϕ+e−iϕ​,sinϕ=2ieiϕ−e−iϕ​.
(15)
Deriving the angle-sum identities from Euler’s formula

Expand ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθe^{i(\omega+\theta)} = e^{i\omega}e^{i\theta}ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθ and match real and imaginary parts:

cos⁡(ω+θ)+isin⁡(ω+θ)=(cos⁡ω+isin⁡ω)(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ).\cos(\omega+\theta) + i\sin(\omega+\theta) = (\cos\omega + i\sin\omega)(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta).cos(ω+θ)+isin(ω+θ)=(cosω+isinω)(cosθ+isinθ).
(16)

Multiplying out the right side and equating parts gives both angle-sum identities at once:

cos⁡(ω+θ)=cos⁡ωcos⁡θ−sin⁡ωsin⁡θ,sin⁡(ω+θ)=sin⁡ωcos⁡θ+cos⁡ωsin⁡θ.\cos(\omega+\theta) = \cos\omega\cos\theta - \sin\omega\sin\theta, \qquad \sin(\omega+\theta) = \sin\omega\cos\theta + \cos\omega\sin\theta.cos(ω+θ)=cosωcosθ−sinωsinθ,sin(ω+θ)=sinωcosθ+cosωsinθ.
(17)

This is why we do not memorize the trig identities separately: they all fall out of one exponential rule. De Moivre’s theorem, (cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)n=cos⁡nθ+isin⁡nθ(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta(cosθ+isinθ)n=cosnθ+isinnθ, follows the same way from (eiθ)n=einθ(e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta}(eiθ)n=einθ.

Where this shows up in chemistry

Complex exponentials are the natural language of anything that oscillates or diffracts. In X-ray crystallography, each scattered wave is a complex number A=∣A∣eiϕA = |A|e^{i\phi}A=∣A∣eiϕ, and the structure factor F(h)=∑jfj eih⋅rjF(\mathbf{h}) = \sum_j f_j\,e^{i\mathbf{h}\cdot\mathbf{r}_j}F(h)=∑j​fj​eih⋅rj​ combines these amplitudes and phases to reconstruct electron density by an inverse Fourier transform. The hard part, the phase problem, is precisely that detectors measure ∣F∣|F|∣F∣ but not the phase ϕ\phiϕ that the complex representation makes explicit.
