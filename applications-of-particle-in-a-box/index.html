Applications of the Particle in a Box

What you will learn

  • The particle in a box (PIB) model is a simplified quantum mechanical system that can be applied to explain electronic transitions in conjugated molecules, such as those with alternating single and double bonds.

  • In these systems, the π-electrons are delocalized and can be approximated as particles confined to a “box” formed by the length of the conjugated region.

Applications in π-Conjugated Systems¶

  • 1D Particle in a Box: Often used for linear conjugated systems like butadiene or polyenes. The box length corresponds to the length of the conjugated chain, and the energy levels are used to predict electronic transitions that occur when light is absorbed.

  • 2D Particle in a Box: For more complex systems like aromatic rings (e.g., benzene) or graphene fragments, the 2D PIB model helps to describe the movement of π-electrons confined to two dimensions.

PIB Applied to Butadiene Electronic Transitions¶

  • Consider the 1D PIB model applied to butadiene (C₄H₆), where the four π-electrons are delocalized over the entire conjugated region. Find the wavelength for exciting one π-electron from the ground state to the next excited state.

  • Single bond (C–C) length: 1.54 Å

  • Double bond (C=C) length: 1.34 Å

  • In this problem, you are tasked with determining both the length of the molecule and the number of π\piπ-electrons in a conjugated system, specifically butadiene. This requires applying the quantum particle-in-a-box (PIB) model to the system.

Step 1: Determine the Length of the Conjugated Region¶

  • The conjugated π\piπ-electrons in butadiene are delocalized across the four carbon atoms, which form a conjugated chain. This conjugated chain can be modeled as a 1D box, with the length of the box corresponding to the length of the conjugated system.

L=1.54 A˚+1.34 A˚+1.54 A˚=4.42 A˚L = 1.54\, \text{Å} + 1.34\, \text{Å} + 1.54\, \text{Å} = 4.42\, \text{Å}L=1.54A˚+1.34A˚+1.54A˚=4.42A˚
(1)

Step 2: Count the Number of π\piπ-Electrons¶

  • In butadiene, the π\piπ-electrons are found in the double bonds between the carbon atoms. Each carbon in the conjugated system contributes one π\piπ-electron, and there are four carbons in the conjugated region. Therefore, there are 4 π\piπ-electrons in total.

Step 3: Apply the Particle-in-a-Box Model¶

Using the 1D PIB model, the energy levels for a particle (i.e., an electron) are given by the equation:

En=n2h28mL2E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}En​=8mL2n2h2​
(2)

Where:

  • nnn is the quantum number (1, 2, 3,...),

  • hhh is Planck’s constant,

  • mmm is the mass of an electron,

  • LLL is the length of the conjugated region (4.42 Å for butadiene).

Each energy level corresponds to a state that the electron can occupy within the box.

Step 4: Calculate the Transition Energy¶

When an electron absorbs energy and transitions between energy levels, the energy difference is given by:

ΔE=En+1−En\Delta E = E_{n+1} - E_nΔE=En+1​−En​
(3)

This energy difference corresponds to the energy of an absorbed photon and can be related to the wavelength of light via:

ΔE=hcλ\Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}ΔE=λhc​
(4)

Where:

  • λ\lambdaλ is the wavelength of the absorbed photon,

  • ccc is the speed of light.

By determining ΔE\Delta EΔE, you can find the corresponding wavelength of light absorbed by the molecule when an electron transitions between energy levels.

  • This approach is used to estimate the energies associated with electronic transitions in π\piπ-conjugated systems like butadiene, which can be experimentally observed through their absorption spectra. This also helps in understanding how molecular size and electron count affect electronic properties.

where L=5×10−10 mL = 5 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}L=5×10−10m, m=9.109×10−31 kgm = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}m=9.109×10−31kg, and h=6.626×10−34 J sh = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}h=6.626×10−34J s. First, calculate ΔE\Delta EΔE, then use the relation:

Problem 2¶

Calculate the total conjugation length of the polyene molecule and the wavelength of light required to excite a π-electron from the n=1n = 1n=1 to n=2n = 2n=2 state using the 1D particle in a box model.

Solution 2

ΔE=3h28mL2\Delta E = \frac{3h^2}{8mL^2}ΔE=8mL23h2​
(8)

Finally, find the wavelength λ\lambdaλ using:

ΔE=hcλ\Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}ΔE=λhc​
(9)

Problem 3¶

Use the particle in a box model to calculate the wavelength of light absorbed during a transition from the n=1n = 1n=1 state to the n=3n = 3n=3 state.

Solution 3

L=4×1.35 A˚+4×1.40 A˚=10.0 A˚=10.0×10−10 mL = 4 \times 1.35 \, \text{Å} + 4 \times 1.40 \, \text{Å} = 10.0 \, \text{Å} = 10.0 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}L=4×1.35A˚+4×1.40A˚=10.0A˚=10.0×10−10m
