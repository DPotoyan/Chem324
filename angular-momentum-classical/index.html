Angular Momentum and Central Forces

What you will learn

  • Angular momentum measures rotational motion. For a particle it is L=r×p\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}L=r×p, and for a rotating body L=IωL = I\omegaL=Iω.

  • Angular momentum is conserved under central forces. A force pointing toward a center exerts no torque, so L\mathbf{L}L stays fixed in size and direction.

  • Central forces reduce to motion in a plane with an effective potential. The angular momentum adds a centrifugal barrier that keeps orbits from collapsing.

  • This is the classical skeleton of the rigid rotor and the hydrogen atom. Quantizing LLL gives rotational energy levels and the angular quantum numbers of atomic orbitals.

Rotation is everywhere in chemistry: molecules tumble, bonds rotate, and electrons orbit nuclei. The classical mechanics of rotation, built on angular momentum and central forces, is the direct ancestor of two quantum models you will study: the rigid rotor and the hydrogen atom. This page develops that classical picture and points to where quantization enters.

Rotational motion and angular momentum¶

For a particle at position r\mathbf{r}r with momentum p=mv\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}p=mv, the angular momentum about the origin is the cross product

Angular momentum of a particle
L=r×p\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}L=r×p
(1)

Its magnitude is L=mvrsin⁡θL = mvr\sin\thetaL=mvrsinθ, and it points perpendicular to the plane of r\mathbf{r}r and v\mathbf{v}v.

For a mass moving in a circle of radius rrr, r\mathbf{r}r and v\mathbf{v}v are perpendicular, so L=mvrL = mvrL=mvr. Writing the speed as v=ωrv = \omega rv=ωr with angular velocity ω\omegaω gives L=mr2ω=IωL = m r^2\omega = I\omegaL=mr2ω=Iω, where

I=mr2I = mr^2I=mr2
(2)

is the moment of inertia, the rotational analogue of mass. The rotational kinetic energy then mirrors the familiar 12mv2\tfrac{1}{2}mv^221​mv2:

Rotational energy
E=12Iω2=L22IE = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{L^2}{2I}E=21​Iω2=2IL2​
(3)

The second form, energy as L2/2IL^2/2IL2/2I, is the one that carries straight into the quantum rigid rotor.

Torque and conservation¶

Just as force changes linear momentum, torque changes angular momentum:

Torque and the rate of change of angular momentum
τ=r×F=dLdt\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}τ=r×F=dtdL​
(4)

A central force points along the line from the center to the particle, so F\mathbf{F}F is parallel to r\mathbf{r}r and their cross product vanishes. With zero torque, angular momentum is conserved:

F∥r  ⟹  τ=0  ⟹  L=constant.\mathbf{F}\parallel\mathbf{r} \;\Longrightarrow\; \boldsymbol{\tau} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathbf{L} = \text{constant}.F∥r⟹τ=0⟹L=constant.
(5)

Gravity and the Coulomb attraction between an electron and a nucleus are both central, so both conserve angular momentum. This is why planetary orbits stay in a plane and sweep out equal areas in equal times (Kepler’s second law), and it is why the electron’s angular momentum is a good quantum number in the hydrogen atom.

Central forces and the effective potential¶

Because L\mathbf{L}L is fixed, motion under a central force stays in a single plane, and the conserved LLL can be folded into the energy. The radial motion then behaves like a one-dimensional problem in an effective potential:

Effective potential for a central force
Veff(r)=V(r)+L22mr2V_{\text{eff}}(r) = V(r) + \frac{L^2}{2mr^2}Veff​(r)=V(r)+2mr2L2​
(6)

The extra term L2/2mr2L^2/2mr^2L2/2mr2 is the centrifugal barrier: it grows without bound as r→0r\to 0r→0, so a particle with any angular momentum is pushed away from the center and cannot fall in. For an attractive Coulomb potential V(r)=−k/rV(r) = -k/rV(r)=−k/r the two terms compete, producing a minimum, a stable orbit at a preferred radius.

The minimum of each curve is a circular orbit; a particle with slightly more energy oscillates in rrr between two turning points, tracing an ellipse. This effective-potential picture reappears almost unchanged in the radial Schrodinger equation for the hydrogen atom, where L2L^2L2 becomes the quantized ℏ2l(l+1)\hbar^2 l(l+1)ℏ2l(l+1).

Where quantization enters¶

Classically LLL can take any value and point in any direction. Quantum mechanics restricts both.

The rigid rotor (Chapter 4). A molecule rotating with fixed bond length has energy E=L2/2IE = L^2/2IE=L2/2I. Quantizing the angular momentum as L2→ℏ2J(J+1)L^2 \to \hbar^2 J(J+1)L2→ℏ2J(J+1) gives the rotational ladder

EJ=ℏ22I J(J+1),J=0,1,2,…E_J = \frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\,J(J+1), \qquad J = 0, 1, 2, \ldotsEJ​=2Iℏ2​J(J+1),J=0,1,2,…
(7)

These are the levels probed by microwave (rotational) spectroscopy.

The hydrogen atom (Chapter 5). The electron feels the central Coulomb force, so its angular momentum is conserved and quantized. The magnitude is set by lll through L=ℏl(l+1)L = \hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)}L=ℏl(l+1)​, and its projection on an axis is set by mmm through Lz=ℏmL_z = \hbar mLz​=ℏm. This “space quantization” of a classically continuous vector is what gives orbitals their shapes and labels.

Quantized angular momentum
L=ℏl(l+1),Lz=ℏm,m=−l,…,+lL = \hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)}, \qquad L_z = \hbar m, \qquad m = -l, \ldots, +lL=ℏl(l+1)​,Lz​=ℏm,m=−l,…,+l
(8)

So the classical conservation law becomes a quantum number, and the classical effective potential becomes the radial equation. The rotational and orbital structure of matter is classical angular momentum, quantized.

Problem 3: Why no torque¶

Explain in one or two sentences why a central force conserves angular momentum.

Solution

A central force is parallel to r\mathbf{r}r, so the torque τ=r×F=0\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} = 0τ=r×F=0. Since dL/dt=τ=0d\mathbf{L}/dt = \boldsymbol{\tau} = 0dL/dt=τ=0, the angular momentum is constant in magnitude and direction.
