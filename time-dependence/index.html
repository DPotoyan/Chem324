Time Dependence

What you need to know

  • The time-dependent Schrödinger equation governs the evolution of the quantum wavefunction over time, ψ(x,t)\psi(x,t)ψ(x,t).

  • Stationary states are eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian H^\hat{H}H^ containing a spatial part and a time-dependent phase factor: ψ(x,t)=ψ(x)e−iEt/ℏ\psi(x,t) = \psi(x) e^{-i E t / \hbar}ψ(x,t)=ψ(x)e−iEt/ℏ

  • The stationary states have definite energy and their probability distributions are time-independent.

  • In more general situations, the wavefunction can be expressed as a linear superposition of energy eigenstates, with time evolution given by the phase factors of the eigenstates: ψ(x,t)=∑ncnψn(x)e−iEnt/ℏ\psi(x,t) = \sum_n c_n \psi_n(x) e^{-i E_n t / \hbar}ψ(x,t)=∑n​cn​ψn​(x)e−iEn​t/ℏ

  • This superposition leads to time-dependent probabilities as different eigenstates interfere.

  • The time-dependence of quantum states is key to understanding quantum dynamics, such as oscillations between states in two-level systems or time evolution of quantum superpositions.

Time Dependence of a Pure Eigenfunction¶

  • Until now, we have largely ignored the time dependence of quantum systems. This is because we have primarily focused on pure eigenfunction states, ψn(x,t)\psi_n(x,t)ψn​(x,t), which exhibit no time-dependent behavior for observable quantities. The reason for this is straightforward.

ψn(x,t)=ψn(x)e−iℏEnt\psi_n(x,t) = \psi_n(x) e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t}ψn​(x,t)=ψn​(x)e−ℏi​En​t
(1)

  • In any probabilistic calculation, the time dependence vanishes because the expressions involve the square of the wavefunction. The absolute value of the complex time-dependent phase factor equals unity:

∣ψn(x,t)∣2=ψn∗(x)ψn(x)e−iℏEnte+iℏEnt=∣ψn(x)∣2\mid \psi_n(x,t) \mid^2 = \psi_n^*(x)\psi_n(x) e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} e^{+\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} = \mid \psi_n(x) \mid^2∣ψn​(x,t)∣2=ψn∗​(x)ψn​(x)e−ℏi​En​te+ℏi​En​t=∣ψn​(x)∣2
(2)

  • Similarly, for the expectation value of an operator A^\hat{A}A^, the time-dependent phases cancel out, leaving only the spatial part:

⟨A⟩=∫ψn∗(x)e+iℏEntA^ψn(x)e−iℏEntdx=∫ψn∗(x)A^ψn(x)dx\langle A \rangle = \int \psi_n^*(x) e^{+\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} \hat{A} \psi_n(x) e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} dx = \int \psi_n^*(x) \hat{A} \psi_n(x) dx⟨A⟩=∫ψn∗​(x)e+ℏi​En​tA^ψn​(x)e−ℏi​En​tdx=∫ψn∗​(x)A^ψn​(x)dx
(3)

  • Will we always have this fortunate cancellation of time dependence? The answer is no. When the system is described as a linear superposition of eigenstates, time dependence reappears in a significant way.

Time Dependence of a Superposition of Eigenfunctions¶

  • Let us start with a superposition state of two energy eigenfunctions at time t=0t=0t=0:

∣ψ(0)⟩=c1∣1⟩+c2∣2⟩\mid \psi(0) \rangle =c_1\mid 1 \rangle + c_2 \mid 2 \rangle∣ψ(0)⟩=c1​∣1⟩+c2​∣2⟩
(4)

  • What would the state be after some time ttt? By virtue of separation of variables, we add the time dependence as a multiplicative factor for each eigenfunction:

∣ψ(t)⟩=c1e−iℏE1t∣1⟩+c2e−iℏE2t∣2⟩=c1(t)∣1⟩+c2(t)∣2⟩\mid \psi(t) \rangle =c_1 e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_1 t}\mid 1 \rangle + c_2 e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_2 t}\mid 2 \rangle= c_1(t)\mid 1\rangle+c_2(t) \mid 2 \rangle∣ψ(t)⟩=c1​e−ℏi​E1​t∣1⟩+c2​e−ℏi​E2​t∣2⟩=c1​(t)∣1⟩+c2​(t)∣2⟩
(5)

  • Thus we see that the coefficients change over time. This means our state vector moves in the functional space spanned by the fixed orthogonal eigenfunctions! We can verify that this expression satisfies the time-dependent Schrödinger equation by plugging it in.

Normalization is time independent!¶

  • Certain quantities remain invariant under time evolution. For instance, it is natural to expect that normalization does not change over time: the particle must always be located somewhere within the box at any given moment.

∣ψ(t)⟩=∑ncne−iℏEnt∣n⟩\mid \psi(t)\rangle = \sum_n c_n e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} \mid n\rangle∣ψ(t)⟩=n∑​cn​e−ℏi​En​t∣n⟩

  • The normalization condition is expressed as:

⟨ψ(t)∣ψ(t)⟩=∑n∑k⟨n∣cn∗eiℏEnt⋅cke−iℏEkt∣k⟩=∑n∑kcn∗cke−iℏ(Ek−En)tδkn=∑n∣cn∣2=1\langle \psi(t) \mid \psi(t)\rangle = \sum_n \sum_k \langle n \mid c^*_n e^{\frac{i}{\hbar}E_n t} \cdot c_k e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}E_k t} \mid k\rangle = \sum_n \sum_k c^*_n c_k e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar}(E_k - E_n)t} \delta_{kn} = \sum_n \mid c_n \mid^2 = 1⟨ψ(t)∣ψ(t)⟩=n∑​k∑​⟨n∣cn∗​eℏi​En​t⋅ck​e−ℏi​Ek​t∣k⟩=n∑​k∑​cn∗​ck​e−ℏi​(Ek​−En​)tδkn​=n∑​∣cn​∣2=1

Constants of Motion¶

  • Quantities that remain conserved over time are called constants of motion. In quantum mechanics, we typically deal with expectations or averages rather than sharply defined values.

  • However, the principle of energy conservation dictates that the average total energy must be a conserved quantity. To determine whether a particular quantity depends on time, we take the time derivative of the expectation value:

    (12)

  • Now, using the time-dependent Schrödinger equation,
